\section{Introduction}
This paper presents a mathematical derivation connecting the Space-Time Equivalence (STE), General Relativity\citep{einstein1915}, and Special Relativity\citep{einstein1905}. We build upon DeGerlia's prior work introducing the concept of Inertial Density and predicting time dilation calculations with exact precision\citep{degerlia202505}, which established that the Schwarzschild condition reduces to a constant threshold of mass over mean radius, the DeGerlia Threshold ($D_{crit} = 6.73295 \times 10^{26}$ kg/m), about the selected axis. From these demonstrations, the mathematical equivalence between the STE, General Relativity, and Special Relativity is established, unifying the two theories under a single framework: Inertial Relativity.

\section{Inertial Relativity}
Inertial Relativity is a principle introduced by the author in a prior paper "The Universe of Light" \citep{degerlia202501}. In that work, we hypothesize that General and Special Relativity emerge from Inertial Relativity. This paper demonstrates that equivalence.

Inertia is relativistic, meaning that without a comparator, any measurement of it would have no meaning. For example, something with a moment of inertia $1 \times 10^{20}$ kg\,m$^2$ might be perceived as difficult to rotate, but not more difficult than something with $1 \times 10^{30}$ kg\,m$^2$. However, the concept of relativity also says that how something is observed is influenced by the frame of observation. Consider for a moment, the following statement that exemplifies our nuanced understanding of the concept of relative scale:
\begin{quote}
``When we were little, adults were big. Now that we're grown up, children seem small.''
\end{quote}
This illustrates that we understand what spatial scale means. We know what relative scale means; we know what it's like to be bigger than something or to be smaller than something. We understand the experience of growing or watching something grow; we know what it is like to change scale relative to something else.

A system, in this context, is any collection of mass and spatial extent you choose to define. It need not be spherical, uniform, high density, or even massive. A system may be a planet, a uniform sphere, a supermassive black hole, a disc galaxy, a cube, a cubic sample of a cumulus cloud, or a system consisting of a hydrogen proton and a single electron.

\subsection{Rotational Inertia}
Moment of inertia for a system is distinct about every axis in the system. For any given axis, the moment of inertia dictates how an applied torque translates into a delta in rotational motion. Moment of inertia is defined as:
\begin{equation}
I = \sum_i m_i r_i^2
\end{equation} 
where $r_i$ is the perpendicular distance from each mass element $m_i$ to the chosen axis. Just as mass governs how a system responds to linear force ($F = ma$), moment of inertia governs how it responds to torque ($\tau = I\alpha$).

\subsection{Linear Inertia}
Linear inertia is mass:
\begin{equation}
M = \sum_i m_i
\end{equation}
where $m_i$ are the constituent mass elements. Linear inertia defines how an applied force translates into a delta in translational motion. It is quantified entirely by mass ($F = ma$). It is dimensional, operating along a specific spatial vector, but unlike moment of inertia, it is isotropic: mass offers the same resistance to changes in motion regardless of the direction of the vector.

\subsection{Isometric Scaling}
Isometric scaling is the uniform scaling of a system in all spatial dimensions while maintaining constant density. Under isometric scaling, every length in the system scales by the same factor $k$:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:length_scaling}
\ell' = k\ell
\end{equation}
where $\ell$ is the characteristic length of the system and $k$ is the linear scale factor. Isometric scaling of a system by a factor of $k$ results in the following changes to mass, radius, and moment of inertia:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:mass_scaling}
M' = k^3 M
\end{equation}
where $M$ is the mass and $k$ is the linear scale factor.

\begin{equation} \label{eq:radius_scaling}
R' = kR
\end{equation}
where $R$ is the mean radius about the selected axis.

\begin{equation} \label{eq:inertia_scaling}
I' = k^5 I
\end{equation}
where $I$ is the moment of inertia about a selected axis; $k$ is the linear scale factor.

\subsection{Inertia Abstracts Mass and Radius}
Because every change in length necessarily changes the system's mass, Equations~\ref{eq:length_scaling}--\ref{eq:radius_scaling} hold only for strict isometric scaling; Equation~\ref{eq:inertia_scaling}, however, abstracts mass and radius into moment of inertia. If two systems share moment of inertia about any axis, then an identical torque applied to each system about that same axis will produce identical changes in motion, regardless of the system geometry; absolutely independent of mass and radius individually. The moment of inertia alone dictates how a system interacts with time about a particular axis. From this, we can conclude the following formula is universal and is not bound in any way to isometric scaling. The derivations that follow demonstrate that General and Special Relativity emerge from this principle.

\subsection{Universality}
The extension from isometrically scaled systems to all systems follows from the nature of moment of inertia itself. Because $I$ fully determines rotational response to applied torque, two systems with the same $I$ are dynamically indistinguishable regardless of geometry. A thin shell and a solid sphere with the same $I$ exhibit the same rotational physics.

The fifth root relationship therefore holds universally: for any two systems about any two respective axes, the characteristic length scale factor that determines time dilation is the fifth root of the inverse ratio of their moments of inertia about those same axes. Isometric scaling establishes the exponent (Equation~\ref{eq:inertia_scaling}).

\subsection{Inertial Density}

Inertial density $P$ is the moment of inertia of a system divided by its volume:
\begin{equation}
P = I/V
\end{equation}
Since linear inertia is mass, this reduces directly to ordinary density $\rho = M/V$ in the linear case; inertial density is the rotational generalization of a quantity already familiar from linear mechanics.

For a uniform sphere, substituting $I = \frac{2}{5}MR^2$ and $V = \frac{4}{3}\pi R^3$, this reduces to:
\begin{equation}
P = \frac{M}{R} \times \frac{3}{10\pi}
\end{equation}
Just as ordinary density ($\rho = M/V$) is mass distributed over volume, inertial density is rotational inertia distributed over volume. As stated in the prior work: \textit{mass and density are to linear motion as moment of inertia and inertial density are to rotational motion.} Because $P$ is defined relative to a selected axis, it can be evaluated per axis directly rather than requiring a full tensor treatment when the quantity varies by direction.

\textbf{DeGerlia Compactness} $D = M/R$ is a simplified analog to inertial density, related to $P$ by the geometric factor $3/10\pi$, and represents the mass over mean radius ratio for a system about the selected axis.

Setting $R = r_s$ (the Schwarzschild condition) \citep{schwarzschild1916radius} and solving yields the \textbf{DeGerlia Threshold}: the universal constant of DeGerlia Compactness at which a system undergoes gravitational collapse:
\begin{equation}
D_{crit} = \frac{c^2}{2G} = 6.73295 \times 10^{26} \text{ kg/m}
\end{equation}
The ratio of a system's DeGerlia Compactness to this threshold gives the time dilation directly:
\begin{equation}
\frac{d\tau}{dt} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{D}{D_{crit}}}
\end{equation}
Substituting $D = M/R$:
\begin{equation}
\frac{d\tau}{dt} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{M}{R \cdot D_{crit}}}
\end{equation}

\section{The Space-Time Equivalence (STE)}
The STE is the core universal equivalence stated by Inertial Relativity:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:ste}
k = \left(\frac{I_2}{I_1}\right)^{1/5}
\end{equation}
where $I_1$ and $I_2$ are the moments of inertia of any two systems about any respective axes. By convention, System 1 denotes the observer and System 2 the observed, consistent with the Schwarzschild convention.

In the linear context, inertia is $M$ and the linear inertia version of the STE applies (see Appendix~\ref{app:general_ste}). Special Relativity describes linear inertia:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:linear}
k = \left(\frac{M_2}{M_1}\right)^{1/3}
\end{equation}
where $M_1$ and $M_2$ are the masses of the two systems.

\subsection{Relating Linear Scale Vector $k$ to General Relativity}
Time dilation is always:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:td_k}
\frac{d\tau}{dt} = \sqrt{1 - k}
\end{equation}
where $d\tau/dt$ is the ratio of proper time to coordinate time and $k$ is the linear scale factor derived from inertia:

\subsection{Properties of Time}
The STE reveals the following properties of time:
\begin{itemize}
\item Time is relativistic: it has meaning only when compared, and how it appears depends on the frame of observation
\item Time emerges from inertia
\item Time is dimensional in three spatial dimensions
\item Time is multimodal: both linear and rotational
\item Time is dynamic for every system or subsystem
\item Time applies to all systems, not only uniform or spherically symmetric ones
\item Time is universally and precisely determined by the spatial distribution of matter in the system, as captured by inertia, about and along every axis, whether or not in motion or being observed
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Physical Interpretation}
The STE states that the relative pace of time for any system about any axis is determined by its moment of inertia about that same axis. The more inertia a system has about that axis, the slower its clock runs relative to a system with less inertia. This is consistent with the notion that time is a measure of change, and inertia resists change. Therefore, the more inertia a system has, the less it changes over a given period of time, and thus the slower its system clock runs relative to a system with less inertia.

At any given time, any system is always undergoing some degree of rotation and some amount of linear motion, however small that is. This law applies to any system whose mass is distributed over some region of space, requiring, at minimum, both mass and spatial extent. One may define the system as the core of a planet, a disc-shaped galaxy, a cosmic filament, or a spherical sample of interstellar space. The per-axis nature of the STE extends to asymmetric systems such as the Kerr metric \citep{kerr1963}, a topic for future investigation.

\subsection{STE Example}
Consider a uniform sphere of radius 1\,m and mass 1\,kg scaled isometrically by a factor of two, producing a second sphere of radius 2\,m and mass $2^3 = 8$\,kg. Their moments of inertia are:
\[
I_1 = \tfrac{2}{5}(8\,\text{kg})(2\,\text{m})^2 = 12.8\,\text{kg\,m}^2
\qquad
I_2 = \tfrac{2}{5}(1\,\text{kg})(1\,\text{m})^2 = 0.4\,\text{kg\,m}^2
\]
The ratio $I_1/I_2 = 32 = 2^5$ confirms that moment of inertia scales as $k^5$ under isometric scaling. Designating the larger sphere as the observer (System~1) and the smaller as the observed (System~2), the STE yields:
\[
k = \left(\frac{I_2}{I_1}\right)^{1/5} = \left(\frac{0.4}{12.8}\right)^{1/5} = \left(\frac{1}{32}\right)^{1/5} = 0.5
\]
recovering the linear scale factor between the two systems. The resulting time dilation is:
\[
\frac{d\tau}{dt} = \sqrt{1 - k} = \sqrt{1 - 0.5} \approx 0.707
\]

\section{General and Special Relativity}
Schwarzschild gravitational time dilation:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:schwarzschild}
\frac{d\tau}{dt} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{r_s}{R}}
\end{equation}
where $r_s = 2GM/c^2$ is the Schwarzschild radius and $R$ is the mean radius about the selected axis.

Lorentz time dilation:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:lorentz}
\frac{d\tau}{dt} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}
\end{equation}
where $v$ is the velocity of the moving frame and $c$ is the speed of light.

These are the same form as Equation~\ref{eq:td_k}, where $k = r_s/R$ in the gravitational case and $k = v^2/c^2$ in the velocity case.


\section{Expressions of Relative Inertial Linear Scale}

The parameter $k$ is always the linear scale factor between two systems about their respective selected axes. Because $k$ is determined by relative inertia, it is fundamentally an inertial ratio. The various scenarios, such as special relativity, gravitational time dilation, etc., are all directly derived from this inertia ratio. In the case of general relativity, we simply set $M_1$ and $M_2$ equal and set $R_2$ to the Schwarzschild radius (see Appendix A: Special-Case Derivations).

Each expression of $k$ below derives from the STE (Equation~\ref{eq:ste}), where $I_1$ and $I_2$ are the moments of inertia of two systems about their respective selected axes. Substituting the moment of inertia for a uniform sphere, $I = \frac{2}{5}MR^2$:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:k_expanded}
k = \left(\frac{\frac{2}{5}M_2 R_2^2}{\frac{2}{5}M_1 R_1^2}\right)^{1/5}
\end{equation}
where $M_1, M_2$ are the masses and $R_1, R_2$ are the mean radii of each system about the selected axis. The geometric constant $\frac{2}{5}$ cancels:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:k_simplified}
k = \left(\frac{M_2 R_2^2}{M_1 R_1^2}\right)^{1/5}
\end{equation}
From here, each derivation applies specific constraints and solves.

\subsection{Static Mass (Gravitational)}
From Equation~\ref{eq:k_simplified}, setting $M_1 = M_2 = M$ and $R_2 = r_s$:
\begin{equation}
k = \left(\frac{M r_s^2}{M \cdot R_1^2}\right)^{1/5}
\end{equation}
where $M$ is the shared mass, $r_s$ is the Schwarzschild radius, and $R$ is the mean radius of the system about the selected axis.
\begin{equation}
k = \left(\frac{r_s^5}{R^5}\right)^{1/5} = \frac{r_s}{R}
\end{equation}
This is equivalently expressed through inertial density or DeGerlia Compactness, since $P \propto M/R$ and $D = M/R$. When mass is static, the ratio of either quantity reduces to the ratio of mean radii about the selected axis:
\begin{equation}
k = \frac{P_1}{P_2} = \frac{D_1}{D_2} = \frac{R_2}{R_1} = \frac{r_s}{R}
\end{equation}
When compared against the DeGerlia Threshold $D_{crit} = 6.73295 \times 10^{26}$ kg/m:
\begin{equation}
k = \frac{D}{D_{crit}}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Static Radius (Linear)}
From Equation~\ref{eq:k_simplified}, when $R_1 = R_2$, the STE reduces to (see \ref{app:general_ste}):
\begin{equation}
k = \left(\frac{M_2}{M_1}\right)^{1/3}
\end{equation}
This is Equation~\ref{eq:linear}. The Lorentz factor (Equation~\ref{eq:lorentz}) has the same form as Equation~\ref{eq:td_k}, with $k = v^2/c^2$. Since gravitational time dilation is equivalently expressed as $r_s/R$ or $v^2/c^2$, the velocity-based $k$ of special relativity and the radius-based $k$ of general relativity are the same length ratio.

\subsection{Constant Density (Isometric)}
When density is held constant, $M \propto R^3$, and the STE reduces to a pure linear scale ratio (see \ref{app:general_ste}):
\begin{equation}
k = \left(\frac{R_2^3 R_2^2}{R_1^3 R_1^2}\right)^{1/5} = \left(\frac{R_2^5}{R_1^5}\right)^{1/5} = \frac{R_2}{R_1}
\end{equation}

\subsection{General Case}
When both mass and radius differ between two systems, no component cancels:
\begin{equation}
k = \left(\frac{I_2}{I_1}\right)^{1/5} = \left(\frac{M_2 R_2^2}{M_1 R_1^2}\right)^{1/5}
\end{equation}
This is the full STE (Equation~\ref{eq:ste}). The static-mass and static-radius cases derived above, producing Schwarzschild and Lorentz time dilation respectively, are edge cases of this formula, each arising when one component of inertia is held constant. The general case applies to any two systems without constraint.

\section{Unification of GR and SR}

From Equation~\ref{eq:td_k}, time dilation is always $d\tau/dt = \sqrt{1-k}$, and $k$ is always the linear scale factor between two systems. In all cases, $k$ reduces to a ratio of characteristic lengths. The Schwarzschild and Lorentz results are not independent discoveries; they are the same expression evaluated under different physical constraints on the same underlying parameter $k$.

When mass is shared between systems, $k = R_2/R_1$, and the STE reduces exactly to Schwarzschild time dilation. When radius is shared, $k = (M_2/M_1)^{1/3}$, and it reduces exactly to Lorentz time dilation. When density is shared, $k = R_2/R_1$ directly. When neither mass, radius, nor density is shared between systems, the full STE applies: $k = (I_2/I_1)^{1/5}$. All three special-case forms reduce to a ratio of characteristic lengths under their respective conditions. Both classical relativistic results emerge from this single expression without additional parameters, geometric similarity, or domain restrictions.

\section{Analysis and Conclusion}

The Space-Time Equivalence (Equation~\ref{eq:td_k}) states that time dilation is:
\[
\frac{d\tau}{dt} = \sqrt{1 - k}
\]
where $k$ is the linear scale factor derived from inertia. Substituting the gravitational case ($k = r_s/R$) directly produces Schwarzschild time dilation (Equation~\ref{eq:schwarzschild}):
\[
\frac{d\tau}{dt} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{r_s}{R}}
\]
Substituting the linear case ($k = v^2/c^2$) directly produces Lorentz time dilation (Equation~\ref{eq:lorentz}):
\[
\frac{d\tau}{dt} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}
\]
Equivalently, $k = D/D_{crit}$ gives the fraction of the DeGerlia Threshold consumed by the system, and is algebraically identical to $r_s/R$.

The STE produces both Schwarzschild and Lorentz time dilation exactly, with no free parameters. Gravitational and kinematic time dilation are not distinct phenomena requiring separate theoretical frameworks. They are the same phenomenon, expressed under different physical constraints, unified by a single inertial parameter whose physical meaning is always and only linear scale. Both are edge cases of the general formula (\ref{app:general_ste}), which is required whenever both mass and radius differ between systems. Because the STE operates on moment of inertia, a quantity defined for any mass distribution about any axis, its applicability is not limited to the symmetric cases treated here. Exploration of asymmetric and higher-order systems is deferred to future work. The framework further suggests that the relationship between time and inertia may hold without bound in both scale and precision, with implications for the unification of phenomena conventionally treated as distinct — subjects to be developed in subsequent work.

\section{Special-Case Derivations} \label{app:general_ste}

The main text presents four expressions of $k$ under different physical conditions (Sections 5.1--5.4). When a component of inertia is held constant between two systems, the exponent of the STE changes from $1/5$ to reflect the degree of homogeneity of the remaining quantity. This appendix derives each case from the general form using Euler's theorem of homogeneous functions \citep{euler1755}, then verifies the general case through decomposition and numerical example.

The fundamental form of the STE is $k = (I_2/I_1)^{1/L}$, where $L$ is the degree of homogeneity of the compared quantity in the characteristic length. Under isometric scaling by factor $k$, a quantity with degree of homogeneity $L$ scales as $k^L$. The specific exponents used throughout this paper ($1/5$ for moment of inertia, $1/3$ for mass, $1/2$ for $R^2$) each emerge from the value of $L$ for that quantity.

\begin{equation}
k = \left(\frac{I_2}{I_1}\right)^{1/L} = \left(\frac{M_2 R_2^2}{M_1 R_1^2}\right)^{1/L}
\end{equation}

$\ell_a/\ell_b$ & Length & 1 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

$I \propto MR^2$ where $R$ is the mean radius about the selected axis.

\textbf{Case 1: Static Mass} $(M_1 = M_2,\ R_1 > R_2)$

\begin{equation}
k = \underbrace{\left(\frac{I_2}{I_1}\right)^{1/L}}_{L=5} = \underbrace{\left(\frac{M R_2^2}{M R_1^2}\right)^{1/L}}_{L=5} = \underbrace{\left(\frac{R_2^2}{R_1^2}\right)^{1/2}}_{L=2} = \underbrace{\frac{R_2}{R_1}}_{L=1}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Case 2: Static Radius} $(R_1 = R_2,\ M_1 > M_2)$

\begin{equation}
k = \underbrace{\left(\frac{I_2}{I_1}\right)^{1/L}}_{L=5} = \underbrace{\left(\frac{M_2 R^2}{M_1 R^2}\right)^{1/L}}_{L=5} = \underbrace{\left(\frac{M_2}{M_1}\right)^{1/3}}_{L=3}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Case 3: Constant Density} $(M \propto R^3,\ R_1 > R_2)$

\begin{equation}
k = \underbrace{\left(\frac{R_2^3 R_2^2}{R_1^3 R_1^2}\right)^{1/5}}_{L=5} = \underbrace{\left(\frac{R_2^5}{R_1^5}\right)^{1/5}}_{L=5} = \underbrace{\frac{R_2}{R_1}}_{L=1}
\end{equation}

\textbf{General Case} $(M_1 \neq M_2,\ R_1 \neq R_2,\ M_1 \neq R_1^3,\ M_2 \neq R_2^3)$

\begin{equation}
k = \underbrace{\left(\frac{I_2}{I_1}\right)^{1/5}}_{L=5} = \underbrace{\left(\frac{M_2 R_2^2}{M_1 R_1^2}\right)^{1/5}}_{L=5}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Verification of Internal Consistency}

To confirm that the general case is consistent with the special cases derived above, we verify that computing $k$ directly from $(I_2/I_1)^{1/5}$ produces the same result as computing the mass contribution (static radius, $L=3$) and radius contribution (static mass, $L=2$) independently and multiplying them.

Starting from $I \propto MR^2$:
\begin{align}
k &= \left(\frac{I_2}{I_1}\right)^{1/5} \notag \\
  &= \left(\frac{M_2 R_2^2}{M_1 R_1^2}\right)^{1/5} \notag \\
  &= \left(\frac{M_2}{M_1} \cdot \frac{R_2^2}{R_1^2}\right)^{1/5} \notag \\
  &= \left(\frac{M_2}{M_1}\right)^{1/5} \cdot \left(\frac{R_2^2}{R_1^2}\right)^{1/5} \notag \\
  &= \left(\frac{M_2}{M_1}\right)^{1/5} \cdot \left(\frac{R_2}{R_1}\right)^{2/5} \notag \\
  &= k_{\text{mass}} \times k_{\text{radius}}
\end{align}

Computed independently for $S_1 = \{M=10^{30}\,\text{kg},\ R=10^{8}\,\text{m}\}$, $S_2 = \{M=10^{20}\,\text{kg},\ R=10^{7}\,\text{m}\}$:

\textbf{Direct, general form:}
\[
k = \left(\frac{10^{20} \cdot (10^{7})^2}{10^{30} \cdot (10^{8})^2}\right)^{1/5} = \left(10^{-12}\right)^{1/5} = 10^{-2.4} = 0.003981
\]

\textbf{Two-step, mass and radius contributions:}

Step 1, mass contribution:
\[
k_{\text{mass}} = \left(\frac{10^{20}}{10^{30}}\right)^{1/5} = \left(10^{-10}\right)^{1/5} = 10^{-2} = 0.01
\]

Step 2, radius contribution:
\[
k_{\text{radius}} = \left(\frac{10^{7}}{10^{8}}\right)^{2/5} = \left(10^{-1}\right)^{2/5} = 10^{-0.4} = 0.39811
\]

Product:
\[
k = k_{\text{mass}} \times k_{\text{radius}} = 0.01 \times 0.39811 = 0.003981
\]

Both paths produce identical results: $d\tau/dt = \sqrt{1 - 0.003981} = 0.998007$, confirming that the general STE is internally consistent.
