\section{Introduction}
This paper presents a mathematical derivation connecting The space-time equivalence (STE)\cite{degerlia2025universe}, General Relativity\cite{einstein1915}, and Special Relativity\cite{einstein1905}. We build upon DeGerlia's prior work introducing the concept of Inertial Density and predicting time dilation calculations with exact precision\cite{degerlia2025}, which established that the Schwarzschild condition reduces to a constant threshold of mass over mean radius - the DeGerlia Threshold: $D_{crit} = 6.73295 \times 10^{26}$ kg/m - about the selected axis. And from these demonstrations the mathematical equivalence between the STE, General Relativity, and Special Relativity is established, unifying the two theories under a single framework: Inertial Relativity.

\section{Inertial Relativity}
Inertial Relativity is a principle introduced by the author in a prior paper "the Universe of Light" \cite{degerlia2025universe}. In that work, we hypothesized that general and special relativity emerge from Inertial Relativity. This paper demonstrates that equivalence.

Inertia is relativistic. Meaning that without a comparator, any measurement of it would have no meaning. For example, something with a moment of inertia $1 \times 10^{20}$ kg/m$^2$ might be perceived as difficult to rotate. But not more difficult than something with $1 \times 10^{30}$ kg/m$^2$. However, the concept of relativity also says that how something is observed is influenced by the frame of observation. Consider for a moment, the following statement that exemplifies our nuanced understanding of the concept of relative scale:
\begin{quote}
``When we were kids, adults looked big. Now that we're adults, kids seem small.''
\end{quote}
This illustrates that we understand what spatial scale means, even though we have not necessarily defined it mathematically. We know what relative scale means; we know what it's like to be bigger than something or to be smaller than something. We understand the experience of growing or watching something grow; we know what it is like to change scale relative to something else.

\subsection{Rotational Inertia}
Rotational Moment of inertia. Moment of inertia for a system is distinct about every axis in the system, and for the axis of rotation that moment of inertia dictates how force translates into a pace of motion. In fact, the axis with the least moment of inertia will be the axis of rotation. It dictates what delta in motion results from an applied torque. Or about the axis of observation? Moment of inertia is defined as:
\begin{equation}
I = \sum_i m_i r_i^2
\end{equation} 
where $r_i$ is the perpendicular distance from each mass element $m_i$ to the chosen axis. Just as mass governs how a body responds to linear force ($F = ma$), moment of inertia governs how it responds to torque ($\tau = I\alpha$).

\subsection{Linear Inertia}
Linear inertia is mass:
\begin{equation}
M = \sum_i m_i
\end{equation}
where $m_i$ are the constituent mass elements.

A body, in this context, is any system of mass you choose to define. Linear inertia is the resistance of a body to changes in its linear velocity, quantified entirely by its mass ($F = ma$). It is dimensional in the sense that it acts along a specific vector --- the direction of the applied force --- but unlike moment of inertia, it is isotropic: mass offers the same resistance to acceleration regardless of which direction that vector points.

\subsection{Isometric Scaling}
Isometric scaling defines $k$ as the linear scale factor:
\begin{equation}
\ell' = k\ell
\end{equation}
where $\ell$ is the characteristic length of the system and $k$ is the linear scale factor. Isometric scaling of a system by a factor of $k$ results in the following changes to mass, radius, and moment of inertia:

\begin{equation}
M' = k^3 M
\end{equation}
where $M$ is the mass and $k$ is the linear scale factor.

\begin{equation}
R' = kR
\end{equation}
where $R$ is the mean radius about the selected axis.

\begin{equation}
I' = k^5 I
\end{equation}
where $I$ is the moment of inertia about a selected axis; $k$ is the linear scale factor.

\begin{equation}
I_{lin}' = M' = k^3 M
\end{equation}
where $I_{lin}$ is linear inertia (mass); $k$ is the linear scale factor.

Because every change in length necessarily changes the system's mass, the first two formulas hold only for strict isometric scaling; the third abstracts mass and radius as moment of inertia. If two systems that share moment of inertia about any axis, then an identical torque applied to each system about that same axis will produce identical changes in motion, regardless of the system geometry that arrived at that moment of inertia; absolutely independent of mass and radius individually. The moment of inertia alone dictates how a system interacts with time about a particular axis. From this, we can conclude the following formula is universal and is not bound in any way to isometric scaling. The derivations that follow demonstrate that general and special relativity emerge from this principle.

\subsection{Universality}
The extension from isometrically scaled systems to all systems follows from the nature of moment of inertia itself. Because $I$ fully determines rotational response to applied torque, two systems with the same $I$ are dynamically indistinguishable regardless of geometry. A thin shell and a solid sphere with the same $I$ exhibit the same rotational physics. Since clock rate is a dynamical observable, it cannot depend on geometric details that $I$ has already absorbed.

The fifth root relationship therefore holds universally: for any two systems, the ratio of their clock rates is the fifth root of the inverse ratio of their moments of inertia. Isometric scaling establishes the exponent (Equation~6). The completeness of $I$ as a dynamical quantity establishes universality. The dynamical nature of clock rate places it within the domain of inertial scaling.

\subsection{Inertial Density}

Inertial density $P$ is the moment of inertia of a system divided by its volume \cite{degerlia2025}:
\begin{equation}
P = I/V
\end{equation}
Since linear inertia is mass, this reduces directly to ordinary density $\rho = M/V$ in the linear case --- inertial density is the rotational generalization of a quantity already familiar from linear mechanics.

For a uniform sphere, substituting $I = \frac{2}{5}MR^2$ and $V = \frac{4}{3}\pi R^3$, this reduces to:
\begin{equation}
P = \frac{M}{R} \times \frac{3}{10\pi}
\end{equation}
Just as ordinary density ($\rho = M/V$) is mass distributed over volume, inertial density is rotational inertia distributed over volume. As stated in \cite{degerlia2025}: \textit{mass and density are to linear motion as moment of inertia and inertial density are to rotational motion.} Because $P$ is defined relative to a selected axis, it can be evaluated per axis directly rather than requiring a full tensor treatment when the quantity varies by direction.

\textbf{DeGerlia Compactness} $D = M/R$ \cite{degerlia2025} is a simplified analog to inertial density, related to $P$ by the geometric factor $3/10\pi$, and represents the mass over mean radius ratio for a system about the selected axis.

Setting $R = r_s$ (the Schwarzschild condition) \cite{schwarzschild1916radius} and solving yields the \textbf{DeGerlia Threshold}: the universal constant of DeGerlia Compactness at which a system undergoes gravitational collapse:
\begin{equation}
D_{crit} = \frac{c^2}{2G} = 6.73295 \times 10^{26} \text{ kg/m}
\end{equation}
The ratio of a system's DeGerlia Compactness to this threshold gives the time dilation directly:
\begin{equation}
\frac{d\tau}{dt} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{D}{D_{crit}}}
\end{equation}

\section{The Space-Time Equivalence (STE)}
The STE is the core universal equivalence stated by Inertial Relativity \cite{degerlia2025universe}. Time dilation is always:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:td_k}
\frac{d\tau}{dt} = \sqrt{1 - k}
\end{equation}
where $d\tau/dt$ is the ratio of proper time to coordinate time and $k$ is the linear scale factor derived from inertia. In the general case:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:ste}
k = \left(\frac{I_2}{I_1}\right)^{1/5}
\end{equation}
where $I_1$ and $I_2$ are the moments of inertia of any two systems about any respective axes. By convention, System 1 denotes the observer and System 2 the observed, consistent with the Schwarzschild convention.

When radius is equal between two systems and cancels from the ratio, inertia reduces to mass:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:linear}
k = \left(\frac{M_2}{M_1}\right)^{1/3}
\end{equation}
where $M_1$ and $M_2$ are the masses of the two systems.

These equations reveal the following about time:
\begin{itemize}
\item It is relativistic, it only has meaning when compared, how it appears depends on the frame of observation
\item It is emergent of inertia
\item It is dimensional in three spatial dimensions
\item It is multimodal: both linear and rotational
\item It is dynamic for every system or subsystem
\item It is infinitely precise
\item It applies to all systems, not only uniform or spherically symmetric ones
\item It is infinite
\item It is universally and precisely determined by the spatial distribution of matter in the system, as captured by inertia, about and along every axis, whether or not in motion or being observed
\end{itemize}

At any given time, any system is always undergoing some degree of rotation and some amount of linear motion, however small that is. It also demonstrates that the basic gravitational time dilation model accommodates for distorted black holes such as the Kerr singularity \cite{kerr1963}. The STE states that the relative pace of time for any system about any axis is determined by its moment of inertia about that same axis. The more inertia a system has about that axis, the slower its clock runs relative to a system with less inertia. This is consistent with the notion that time is a measure of change, and inertia resists change. Therefore, the more inertia a system has, the less it changes over a given period of time, and thus the slower its system clock runs relative to a system with less inertia. This law applies to any system whose mass is distributed over some region of space --- requiring, at minimum, both mass and spatial extent. One may define the system as the core of a planet, a disc-shaped galaxy, a cosmic filament, or a spherical sample of interstellar space.

\subsection{STE Example}
If one takes a uniform sphere of 1 m in radius and 1 kg of mass and scales it up by a factor of two, the resulting system is 8 kg of mass with a radius of 2 m. Moment of inertia of the initial system is:
\[
\frac{2}{5} \cdot 1\,\text{kg} \cdot (1\,\text{m})^2 = 0.4\,\text{kg\,m}^2
\]
That larger scale system has a mass that is the cube of the scale factor multiplied by the original mass $2^3 \cdot 1\,\text{kg} = 8\,\text{kg}$, and would have a moment of inertia that is the fifth power of the scale factor times the original moment of inertia. The inertial scale factor is $k^5 = 2^5 = 32$. Conservation of momentum gives the best representation of how changes in moment of inertia affect motion. The moment of inertia for the scaled system calculates to:
\[
\frac{2}{5}(8\,\text{kg})(2\,\text{m})^2 = \frac{2}{5} \cdot 8\,\text{kg} \cdot 4\,\text{m}^2 = 0.4(32)
\]
resulting in the predicted factor of two to the fifth power, or 32. If our sphere were rotating at one meter per second initially, the scaled system would now be moving half of that (0.5 m/s) resulting from the $2^5 = 32\times$ increased moment of inertia.

\section{General and Special Relativity}
Schwarzschild gravitational time dilation \cite{schwarzschild1916radius}:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:schwarzschild}
\frac{d\tau}{dt} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{r_s}{R}}
\end{equation}
where $r_s = 2GM/c^2$ is the Schwarzschild radius and $R$ is the mean radius about the selected axis.

Lorentz time dilation \cite{einstein1905}:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:lorentz}
\frac{d\tau}{dt} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}
\end{equation}
where $v$ is the velocity of the moving frame and $c$ is the speed of light.

These are the same form as Equation~\ref{eq:td_k}, where $k = r_s/R$ in the gravitational case and $k = v^2/c^2$ in the velocity case.

\section{Expressions of Relative Inertial Linear Scale}

The parameter $k$ is always the linear scale factor between two systems about their respective selected axes. Because $k$ is determined by inertia, it is always an inertial ratio. What varies across the expressions below is which components of inertia are already known or fixed (see Appendix~\ref{app:general_ste} for the general derivation).

Each expression of $k$ below derives from the STE (Equation~\ref{eq:ste}), where $I_1$ and $I_2$ are the moments of inertia of two systems about their respective selected axes. Substituting the moment of inertia for a uniform sphere, $I = \frac{2}{5}MR^2$:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:k_expanded}
k = \left(\frac{\frac{2}{5}M_2 R_2^2}{\frac{2}{5}M_1 R_1^2}\right)^{1/5}
\end{equation}
where $M_1, M_2$ are the masses and $R_1, R_2$ are the mean radii of each system about the selected axis. The geometric constant $\frac{2}{5}$ cancels:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:k_simplified}
k = \left(\frac{M_2 R_2^2}{M_1 R_1^2}\right)^{1/5}
\end{equation}
From here, each derivation applies specific constraints and solves.

\subsection{Static Mass (Gravitational)}
From Equation~\ref{eq:k_simplified}, when $M_1 = M_2$, mass cancels from the ratio. The remaining quantity is $R^2$, so:
\begin{equation}
k = \left(\frac{R_2^2}{R_1^2}\right)^{1/2} = \frac{R_2}{R_1}
\end{equation}
Setting $R_2 = r_s$:
\begin{equation}
k = \frac{r_s}{R}
\end{equation}
This is equivalently expressed through inertial density \cite{degerlia2025} or DeGerlia Compactness, since $P \propto M/R$ and $D = M/R$. When mass is static, the ratio of either quantity reduces to the ratio of mean radii about the selected axis:
\begin{equation}
k = \frac{P_1}{P_2} = \frac{D_1}{D_2} = \frac{R_2}{R_1} = \frac{r_s}{R}
\end{equation}
When compared against the DeGerlia Threshold $D_{crit} = 6.73295 \times 10^{26}$ kg/m:
\begin{equation}
k = \frac{D}{D_{crit}}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Static Radius (Linear)}
From Equation~\ref{eq:k_simplified}, when $R_1 = R_2$, the $R^2$ component cancels from the ratio. The remaining quantity is mass:
\begin{equation}
k = \left(\frac{M_2}{M_1}\right)^{1/3}
\end{equation}
This is Equation~\ref{eq:linear}. The Lorentz factor (Equation~\ref{eq:lorentz}) has the same form as Equation~\ref{eq:td_k}, with:
\begin{equation}
k = \frac{v^2}{c^2}
\end{equation}
From:
\[
v^2 = \frac{2GM}{r}, \qquad r_s = \frac{2GM}{c^2}
\]
it follows that:
\begin{equation}
\frac{v^2}{c^2} = \frac{r_s}{r}
\end{equation}
The velocity-based $k$ of special relativity and the radius-based $k$ of general relativity are the same length ratio.

\subsection{Constant Density (Isometric)}
When density is held constant, $M \propto R^3$, and the STE reduces directly to a pure linear scale ratio:
\begin{equation}
k = \left(\frac{R_2^3 R_2^2}{R_1^3 R_1^2}\right)^{1/5} = \left(\frac{R_2^5}{R_1^5}\right)^{1/5} = \frac{R_2}{R_1}
\end{equation}

\subsection{General Case}
When both mass and radius differ between two systems, no component cancels:
\begin{equation}
k = \left(\frac{I_2}{I_1}\right)^{1/5} = \left(\frac{M_2 R_2^2}{M_1 R_1^2}\right)^{1/5}
\end{equation}
This is the full STE (Equation~\ref{eq:ste}). The static-mass and static-radius cases derived above --- producing Schwarzschild and Lorentz time dilation respectively --- are edge cases of this formula, each arising when one component of inertia is held constant. The general case applies to any two systems without constraint.

\section{Unification of GR and SR}

From Equation~\ref{eq:td_k}, time dilation is always $d\tau/dt = \sqrt{1-k}$, and $k$ is always the linear scale factor between two systems. In all cases, $k$ reduces to a ratio of characteristic lengths. The Schwarzschild and Lorentz results are not independent discoveries --- they are the same expression evaluated under different physical constraints on the same underlying parameter $k$.

In the general case, $k = (I_2/I_1)^{1/5}$ (Equation~\ref{eq:ste}). When mass is static, it cancels and $k = R_2/R_1 = r_s/R$. When radius is static, it cancels and $k = (M_2/M_1)^{1/3} = v^2/c^2$ (Equation~\ref{eq:linear}). General relativity is the static-mass case; special relativity is the static-radius case. Both are edge cases of the general formula, which applies whenever both mass and radius differ between two systems. The distinction between the two theories maps directly onto which component of moment of inertia has been held constant.

\section{Analysis and Conclusion}

The Space-Time Equivalence (Equation~\ref{eq:td_k}) states that time dilation is:
\[
\frac{d\tau}{dt} = \sqrt{1 - k}
\]
where $k$ is the linear scale factor derived from inertia. Substituting the gravitational case ($k = r_s/R$) directly produces Schwarzschild time dilation (Equation~\ref{eq:schwarzschild}):
\[
\frac{d\tau}{dt} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{r_s}{R}}
\]
Substituting the linear case ($k = v^2/c^2$) directly produces Lorentz time dilation (Equation~\ref{eq:lorentz}):
\[
\frac{d\tau}{dt} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}
\]
Equivalently, $k = D/D_{crit}$ gives the fraction of the DeGerlia Threshold consumed by the system, and is algebraically identical to $r_s/R$.

The STE produces both Schwarzschild and Lorentz time dilation exactly, with no free parameters. Gravitational and kinematic time dilation are not distinct phenomena requiring separate theoretical frameworks. They are the same phenomenon, expressed under different physical constraints, unified by a single inertial parameter whose physical meaning is always and only linear scale. Both are edge cases of the general formula (Appendix~\ref{app:general_ste}), which applies whenever both mass and radius differ between systems. The equivalence with the Schwarzschild solution \cite{schwarzschild1916radius} constitutes equivalence with the Einstein field equations \cite{einstein1915} by transitivity.

k = \left(\frac{I_2}{I_1}\right)^{1/L} = \left(\frac{m_s r_2^2}{m_s r_1^2}\right)^{1/L} = \left(\frac{r_2^2}{r_1^2}\right)^{1/2} = \frac{r_2}{r_1}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Case 2 --- Static Radius} $(r_1 = r_2 = r_s,\ m_1 \neq m_2)$

\begin{equation}
k = \left(\frac{I_2}{I_1}\right)^{1/L} = \left(\frac{m_2 r_s^2}{m_1 r_s^2}\right)^{1/L} = \left(\frac{m_2}{m_1}\right)^{1/3}
\end{equation}

\textbf{General Case} $(m_1 \neq m_2,\ r_1 \neq r_2)$

\begin{equation}
k = \left(\frac{I_2}{I_1}\right)^{1/5} = \left(\frac{m_2 r_2^2}{m_1 r_1^2}\right)^{1/5}
\end{equation}

\subsection*{Verification}

The general case decomposes exactly into its mass and radius contributions:

Starting from $I = mr^2$:
\begin{align}
k &= \left(\frac{I_2}{I_1}\right)^{1/5} \notag \\
  &= \left(\frac{m_2 r_2^2}{m_1 r_1^2}\right)^{1/5} \notag \\
  &= \left(\frac{m_2}{m_1} \cdot \frac{r_2^2}{r_1^2}\right)^{1/5} \notag \\
  &= \left(\frac{m_2}{m_1}\right)^{1/5} \cdot \left(\frac{r_2^2}{r_1^2}\right)^{1/5} \notag \\
  &= \left(\frac{m_2}{m_1}\right)^{1/5} \cdot \left(\frac{r_2}{r_1}\right)^{2/5} \notag \\
  &= k_{\text{mass}} \times k_{\text{radius}}
\end{align}

Computed independently for $S_1 = \{m=10^{30}\,\text{kg},\ r=10^{8}\,\text{m}\}$, $S_2 = \{m=10^{20}\,\text{kg},\ r=10^{7}\,\text{m}\}$:

\textbf{Direct --- general form:}
\[
k = \left(\frac{10^{20} \cdot (10^{7})^2}{10^{30} \cdot (10^{8})^2}\right)^{1/5} = \left(10^{-12}\right)^{1/5} = 10^{-2.4} = 0.003981
\]

\textbf{Two-step --- mass and radius contributions:}

Step 1 --- mass contribution:
\[
k_{\text{mass}} = \left(\frac{10^{20}}{10^{30}}\right)^{1/5} = \left(10^{-10}\right)^{1/5} = 10^{-2} = 0.01
\]

Step 2 --- radius contribution:
\[
k_{\text{radius}} = \left(\frac{10^{7}}{10^{8}}\right)^{2/5} = \left(10^{-1}\right)^{2/5} = 10^{-0.4} = 0.39811
\]

Product:
\[
k = k_{\text{mass}} \times k_{\text{radius}} = 0.01 \times 0.39811 = 0.003981
\]

Both paths produce identical results: $d\tau/dt = \sqrt{1 - 0.003981} = 0.998007$, confirming that the general STE is internally consistent.
